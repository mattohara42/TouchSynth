who makes music like this? 🎶

Grid Sings is a homage to the Yamaha Tenori-on, a glowing grid instrument where anyone — especially kids — can make real music with no wrong notes. The patterns it plays have a long family tree. Each name links to a listen.

the family tree

Where playable, glowing, no-wrong-notes music machines come from.

  • Toshio IwaiMedia artist who designed the actual Tenori-on (and Electroplankton on the Nintendo DS). This toy has an inventor.
  • Laurie SpiegelBuilt Music Mouse in the 1980s — an "intelligent instrument" for people with no training. The original no-wrong-notes philosophy.
  • Brian EnoInvented ambient music; his Bloom app — tap to place looping tones — is this instrument's closest living cousin.
  • KraftwerkGrandfathers of the grid. Every step sequencer traces back to them — "Pocket Calculator" is literally a song about a musical toy.

the sound

Interlocking mallet loops — what the grid actually sounds like.

  • Steve ReichThe interlocking patterns you just made? He built a career on exactly that. Start with "Six Marimbas", then "Music for 18 Musicians".
  • Terry Riley"In C" — short melodic loops layered until they shimmer. It's even in the right key.
  • GamelanIndonesia's bronze-mallet orchestras: pentatonic, looping, communal, centuries old. The deepest root of all of this.

the warmth

Electronic music with a childhood glow.

  • Boards of CanadaWarm, wobbly loops that sound like a half-remembered kids' TV show. Start with "Roygbiv".
  • Mort Garson"Plantasia" — Moog lullabies written for houseplants, in 1976.
  • Raymond ScottMade electronic music for infants in 1962, before almost anyone had heard a synthesizer.
  • LullatoneA duo in Japan making "pajama pop" on toy instruments. Maybe the most on-theme act alive.
  • Plaid"Eyen" — a music-box melody from the same family as this grid's voice.
  • Sakamoto / YMOJapanese electro-pop pioneers on Yamaha machines — the same company that built the Tenori-on.
  • Aphex Twin"Flim" — the electronic track children reliably love. Then "Avril 14th".
  • Squarepusher"Tommib" — ninety gentle seconds you may know from Lost in Translation.
  • AutechreMachines making patterns no hand would play — the spirit of Bounce mode. Start gently, with "Bike".
